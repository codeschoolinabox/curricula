# 4.6: Vibecoding

The anti-pattern that proves the chapter's thesis. Mirrors how each chapter ends
with a paradigm flip (Ch 3.6: plaintext programs strip away IDE assistance).

## Part 1: Constrained Vibecoding

Vibecode a program within the constraints of previous chapters — use only the
structure, complexity, and language features from Chs 1–3. Let the LLM drive,
but within bounds.

This is deliberately revealing:

- Can learners actually understand the generated code? (If the LLM uses features
  they haven't learned, they can't evaluate it.)
- How much does the LLM overcomplicate things within "simple" constraints?
- How much trouble does the LLM have sticking to constraints at all?
- The constrained scope means learners CAN do the autopsy — they have the skills
  to fully evaluate the output.

## Part 2: Open-Ended Vibecoding

Now vibecode something fun and ambitious with no constraints. Let the LLM drive
completely. No careful prompting, no evaluation, no theory of mind. Just vibes.
Compare the experience to Part 1.

## The Autopsy

Forensically analyze the results of both parts using every skill from the
chapter:

- **Perspective-Take:** What did the LLM "understand" about your vibe? What
  patterns did it match?
- **Calibrate:** Where did the jagged frontier show up? What worked surprisingly
  well? What failed?
- **Read/Trace:** Can you follow the generated code? Trace it? Predict its
  behavior?
- **Describe (PBSI):** Can you articulate Purpose, Behavior, Strategy,
  Implementation for code you didn't write or evaluate?
- **Gell-Mann Amnesia moment:** The parts that look right... are they actually
  right? How would you know?
- **Delegate reflection:** What would you have done differently with the
  collaboration skills you now have?

## Optional Extension: The Gell-Mann Test

The autopsy above tests your comprehension of code you didn't write — but you're
working in your area of developing expertise. The Gell-Mann Amnesia effect
predicts that you're much worse at spotting errors _outside_ your expertise,
even though the errors look just as confident.

Try this: ask the LLM to explain something in a domain you _don't_ know well (a
chemistry concept, a historical claim, a legal principle). Analyze the output
with the same critical eye. Can you spot the errors? Compare your confidence to
how you felt analyzing the generated code. The gap is the Gell-Mann effect in
action.

## The Punchline

Vibecoding isn't inherently bad — but without comprehension skills and a working
model of the collaborator, you can't tell when it works and when it doesn't.
This chapter gave you the tools to know the difference.

## Skills and Objectives

- 🐣 Decompose a complex request into smaller, verifiable steps (by contrast
  with vibecoding's all-at-once approach)
- 🐥 Evaluate code you didn't write or evaluate during generation (PBSI autopsy)
- 🐥 Identify where the jagged frontier manifested in a concrete collaboration
- 🐔 Reflect on the difference between "it runs" and "I understand it"
