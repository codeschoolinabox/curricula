# 4.0: What Is an LLM?

Before any LLM interaction, learners build a mental model of what they'll be
collaborating with. The goal is not technical mastery of neural networks — it's
developing enough understanding to predict, evaluate, and reason about LLM
behavior.

Neuroscience and communication research tell us that successful collaboration
between any two agents depends on **shared generative models** — each agent's
internal model of how the other thinks and communicates.[^shared-models] When
two humans talk, both sides adapt their models in real time. With an LLM, the
collaboration is asymmetric: the LLM's model is fixed (it was set during
training), so **you** have to do almost all the model-building
work.[^asymmetric] That's what the 4 Levels below are for — they're not just
"background knowledge about AI." They're the foundation of your half of the
shared model.

[^shared-models]:
    [🔬](./research-framing.md#shared-models) Research on how agents communicate
    suggests that successful collaboration depends on each side building a
    working model of how the other thinks and responds. Clark (1996) calls this
    "common ground"; Friston & Frith (2015) formalize it as "active inference."

[^asymmetric]:
    [📐](./research-framing.md#asymmetric-duet) In human-human conversation,
    both sides adapt their models of each other. With an LLM, only the human
    adapts — the LLM's model was set during training. We call this the
    "asymmetric duet" (our translation of Friston & Frith's framework to the LLM
    context).

## The 4 Levels of AI Abstraction

A framework for building your mental model of the collaborator, one layer at a
time. All four levels are introduced for context; Levels 1–3 are the
focus.[^4-levels-grounding]

[^4-levels-grounding]:
    [🔬](./research-framing.md#skill-ordering) The 4 Levels operationalize a
    well-established progression in cognitive science: from understanding an
    individual system (Dennett's three stances, 1987; Marr's levels of
    analysis, 1982) to understanding the interaction system that forms when two
    agents collaborate (distributed cognition, Hutchins 1995; interactive team
    cognition, Cooke et al. 2013; shared generative models, Friston & Frith
    2015). Levels 0–2 build your model of the agent; Level 3 addresses what
    emerges when you and the agent interact — a shift from individual to dyadic
    analysis. Shanahan (2023) bridges these frameworks to LLMs specifically.

**Level 0 — Physical:** The hardware and infrastructure that runs LLMs. Data
centers, GPUs, energy costs, training data pipelines. Important context for the
societal picture, but not the focus here. Mention, move on.

**Level 1 — Conceptual:** How LLMs work, mechanistically.

The starting point: LLMs predict the next token based on statistical patterns in
training data. This is why the same prompt can produce different outputs — the
model samples from probability distributions, not looking up answers in a
database. This single fact kills the most common misconception (keyword lookup /
database retrieval).

But "just token prediction" is also an oversimplification. Mechanistic
interpretability research reveals that LLMs develop discrete, manipulable
internal representations of concepts — not just statistical correlations:

- Anthropic's "Scaling Monosemanticity" (2024) found that individual features
  inside Claude correspond to specific concepts. The Golden Gate Bridge Claude
  experiment demonstrated this: amplifying the "Golden Gate Bridge" feature made
  the model relate everything to the bridge. Specific features = specific
  concepts, and they can be turned up or down.
- Anthropic's circuit tracing (2025) revealed multilingual "language of thought"
  inside the model, planning behavior for poetry, and distinguishable genuine
  vs. fabricated chain-of-thought.
- The Linear Representation Hypothesis (Park et al., ICML 2024): concepts are
  directions in high-dimensional space. Related concepts cluster geometrically.
  This is unlike anything in human cognition, but it's more than "just
  statistics."

The bridge to Level 2: It's not looking up answers, AND it's not just predicting
tokens mechanically — it builds internal representations of concepts. But those
representations are fundamentally different from human understanding. That's
what Level 2 explores.

**Level 2 — "Cognitive":** What LLMs "know" and how they "think." The alien
cognition frame.

LLMs form something like concepts (Level 1 showed this), but they don't
"understand" them the way humans do. Functional representations without
human-style comprehension. The emerging middle ground in the scientific debate:
not "stochastic parrots" (Bender & Koller, 2020), not human-like understanding,
but something in between that we don't yet have good vocabulary for.

Key findings that shape this picture:

- **Truth has geometry.** Marks & Tegmark's "Geometry of Truth" (2023) shows
  truth and falsehood are linearly separable in LLM activation space. The
  model's internal representations of true statements cluster separately from
  false ones.
- **"Knowing" without saying.** Orgad et al. (ICLR 2025) shows LLMs sometimes
  encode correct answers internally but fail to output them. Hallucination isn't
  always "doesn't know" — sometimes it's "knows but doesn't say."
- **Lying ≠ hallucinating.** Huan et al. (2025) shows these are mechanistically
  distinct inside the model. Different pathways, different causes.
- **World models exist.** Li et al. (ICLR 2025) trained models on Othello game
  moves and found they developed 99% accurate internal world models across 7
  architectures — the model "knows" the board state, not just the sequence of
  moves.

Two useful frames for thinking about this:

- **The role-play frame** (Shanahan, Nature 2023, CACM 2024): "The model
  simulates a superposition of plausible characters." Not one mind, not a
  database — a simulator of possible respondents.
- **The swarm, not the individual** (Vanhoucke): Millions of perspectives, no
  singular viewpoint. Prompting selects which "constituency" gets amplified.

For our purposes: treat it as genuinely alien cognition. Not "smart human." Not
"dumb database." Something else entirely — and building a theory of mind for
that something else is the foundation of collaboration.

**Level 3 — Collaborative:** What emerges when you and the LLM interact — the
dynamics of collaboration.[^level3-rename]

[^level3-rename]:
    The shift from Level 2 (the LLM in isolation) to Level 3 (the human-LLM
    interaction) mirrors a fundamental progression in cognitive science: from
    individual cognition to interactive/distributed cognition (Hutchins 1995,
    Cooke et al. 2013). We call this level "Collaborative" rather than
    "Behavioral" to make the unit-of-analysis shift explicit — Level 3 is about
    the dyad, not just the agent.

- **Stochasticity:** Same input → different output. The model samples from
  distributions, so you never get exactly the same response twice.
- **The jagged frontier** (Mollick, "Co-Intelligence"): LLM capabilities are
  irregular. It might ace a complex task and fail a simple one. You cannot
  extrapolate from one success to another — test empirically.
- **"Chemistry, not conversation":** Small prompt changes → wildly different
  outputs. The dynamics of interaction shape results more than any individual
  prompt.
- **Hallucination / confident incorrectness:** The model produces wrong
  information with the same confidence as correct information. This is
  mechanistically distinct from "lying" (Level 2) — it's a different failure
  mode.
- **The "third thing"**: Something emerges from human-AI collaboration that
  neither party produces alone. Established research shows collective
  intelligence is distinct from individual intelligence (Woolley et al. 2010,
  🔬) and that human-AI complementarity depends on more than raw ability
  (Steyvers et al. 2022, 🔬). Riedl & Weidmann (2025, preprint, 📐) extend this
  to suggest perspective-taking — building a mental model of the AI — may be a
  key differentiator for collaboration quality. Vaccaro et al. (2024, 🔬) add
  that synergy is task-dependent: positive for creation tasks, absent or
  negative for decision-making.
- **Gell-Mann Amnesia effect:** You can spot LLM errors in your area of
  expertise. In unfamiliar areas, the errors look just as confident. The
  implication: you should never fully trust LLM output in domains where you
  can't independently evaluate it.

## Skills and Objectives

- 🥚 Explain why an LLM is not a database or keyword-lookup system
- 🥚 Describe what "predicting the next token" means in practical terms
- 🥚 Explain why the same prompt can produce different outputs (stochasticity)
- 🐣 Describe at least 2 key differences between LLM "cognition" and human
  cognition
- 🐣 Identify when an LLM is likely to be unreliable (jagged frontier)
- 🐥 Use the 4 Levels of Abstraction framework to discuss AI at appropriate
  depth
- 🐥 Explain the Gell-Mann Amnesia effect in the context of LLM output

## Key Misconceptions

| Misconception                                               | Why It Fails                                                                                       | Better Mental Model                                                        |
| ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "It looks up answers in a database"                         | Can't explain stochasticity or hallucination                                                       | It predicts tokens AND builds non-human concept representations            |
| "It understands my code"                                    | Can't explain inconsistent bug detection                                                           | It has functional representations, not human understanding                 |
| "Same prompt = same result"                                 | Contradicted by experience                                                                         | Sampling from probability distributions                                    |
| "If it got one thing right, it'll get similar things right" | The jagged frontier                                                                                | Capabilities are irregular — test empirically                              |
| "It's like a very smart person"                             | Anthropomorphizing leads to wrong predictions                                                      | It's a genuinely different kind of intelligence — build a new mental model |
| "When it's wrong, it doesn't know the answer"               | Orgad et al. shows internal truth encoding                                                         | Sometimes it "knows" but fails to output correctly                         |
| "Good prompts are all that matter"                          | Woolley 2010, Steyvers 2022, 📐 Riedl 2025 (preprint): collaboration dynamics > individual prompts | The collaboration dynamic matters more than any single prompt              |

## Exercise Types

- Discussion: What do you think happens when you type a question into ChatGPT?
  (Surface existing mental models)
- Misconception identification: Given a list of claims about LLMs, identify
  which are accurate and which are misconceptions
- Analogies: "An LLM is like a \_\_\_" — generate analogies, then stress-test
  them. What does your analogy predict correctly? What does it get wrong?
- Golden Gate Bridge Claude case study: Read about the experiment, discuss what
  it reveals about internal representations
