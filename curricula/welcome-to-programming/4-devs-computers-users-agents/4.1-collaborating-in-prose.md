# 4.1: Collaborating in Prose

Practice all new collaboration skills in pure natural language first, discussing subjects you are already familiar with and have nothing to do with code. Many key misconceptions can be surfaced and addressed before code enters the picture.

## Skills and Objectives

- 🥚 Given an LLM response, hypothesize what patterns it might be matching (**Perspective-Take**)
- 🥚 Write clear, specific prompts that provide necessary context (**Articulate**)
- 🐣 Ask the same question multiple ways and observe how outputs vary (**Calibrate**)
- 🐣 When a response isn't useful, identify what to change and observe the effect (**Iterate**)
- 🐣 Predict how changes to a prompt will affect LLM output, and test the prediction
- 🐥 Explain why an LLM produced incorrect or unexpected output
- 🐥 Reflect on when it helped to let the LLM lead vs. when you needed to drive (**SOLO awareness**)

## Collaboration Strategies (Index Table)

A reference table of prose collaboration strategies, introduced here and expanded through subsequent subchapters:

| Strategy                     | When to Use                             | What It Tests                                   |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Ask it to explain            | When you don't understand something     | Your ability to evaluate the explanation        |
| Ask it to wear a hat         | When you want a specific perspective    | Whether the perspective is authentic/useful     |
| Request adversarial review   | When you want to stress-test an idea    | Your ability to evaluate critique quality       |
| Rephrase your question       | When the response misses your intent    | Your articulation skills                        |
| Ask what it needs to know    | When starting a complex task            | Its "awareness" of context gaps                 |
| Provide explicit constraints | When output is too broad or wrong scope | How well it respects constraints (often poorly) |

(This table grows as code-specific strategies are added in subchapters 4.2–4.4.)

## Exercise Types

- **Perspective-Take exercises:** Ask the LLM a question, study the response, then answer: "Why did it say _that_? What patterns might it be matching? What context did it lack?"
- **Calibrate exercises:** Ask the same question multiple ways. Ask about topics you know well vs. topics you don't. When can you spot errors? When can't you?
- **Gell-Mann Amnesia awareness:** Discuss: you can spot LLM errors in your expertise area. In unfamiliar areas, the errors look just as confident. What does this mean for how you should use LLM output?
- **Voice-switching exercise:** Switch your writing voice mid-conversation (formal → casual, technical → poetic) and observe how the LLM mirrors your tone, vocabulary, and style. Demonstrates Level 3 collaborative patterns (the "swarm" selecting a new constituency) and the "third thing" — the collaboration dynamic shapes the output more than any single prompt.
- **Hat-wearing and adversarial reviews:** Explicitly ask the LLM to wear different hats (critic, teacher, devil's advocate). Observe how asking for adversarial review produces different output than asking for help. A key collaboration strategy applicable throughout the curriculum.
- **Articulate exercises:** Practice being specific, providing context, decomposing questions. Compare vague vs. precise prompts and observe the difference in output quality.
- **Iterate exercises:** Start with a prompt, evaluate the response, modify the prompt, re-evaluate. Track what changed and why. The goal: develop intuition for how the collaboration loop works.
- **SOLO reflection (woven in):** After each exercise, reflect: "Am I building structure (learning how to collaborate) or leveraging structure (using the LLM productively)?" Different goals → different collaboration approaches. Connection to studying-with-llms strategies from earlier chapters — reframing with theory of mind.
