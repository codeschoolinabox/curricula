# Foreword

## For Learners

This curriculum teaches you to program — and to work effectively with AI while
you learn.

Most "learn to code" resources either ignore AI entirely or throw you into it
without preparation. We take a different approach: you build real programming
skills first (reading, tracing, understanding code), then learn how to
collaborate with AI tools in a way that strengthens those skills instead of
replacing them.

**What to expect:**

- Chapters 0–3 build your foundation: how to read code, understand what the
  computer does, and communicate with other developers and users
- Chapter 4 introduces AI as a collaborator — not just "use ChatGPT," but a
  framework for understanding what AI can and can't do, and how to work with it
  productively
- Chapters 5–6 apply everything to algorithms and problem-solving

**You might notice** small linked emoji (🔬, 📐, 🧪) scattered through the
content. These are evidence tags — they tell educators and researchers how
confident we are in each teaching choice. You can safely ignore them. They won't
get in your way, and they're not part of what you need to learn.

**The one thing we ask:** give us feedback. Tell us what worked, what didn't,
where you got stuck, where you got excited. This curriculum improves through
use.

## For Educators

This curriculum is evidence-informed. Every major pedagogical choice has a
traceable rationale, and we're honest about how strong that rationale is.

### Evidence Tags

Throughout the content, you'll see small linked emoji:

- 🔬 **Established** — Direct research backing. Peer-reviewed, replicated or
  highly cited.
- 📐 **Translated** — Established theory applied to a new context. The original
  finding is solid; the application here is our informed conjecture.
- 🧪 **Extension** — Derived from experience, extrapolation, or untested
  prediction. We think this is right, but it hasn't been tested yet.

Clicking an evidence tag takes you to the chapter's `research-framing.md`, which
explains the evidence behind that specific claim.

### Per-Chapter Research Framing

Each chapter has a `research-framing.md` file that explains:

- The evidence base for the chapter's pedagogical design
- Which research questions the chapter's exercises help investigate
- How the chapter connects to the broader theoretical framework

Chapter 4's research framing is the most developed. Other chapters have stubs
that will grow as they mature.

### Design Principle

When we face a choice between what's best for learning and what's best for
research observability, learning wins. Always. Exercises are designed for
learners first; any research value comes from natural byproducts of the learning
process, not from extra instrumentation.

### For Researchers

If you're interested in the research dimensions of this curriculum — the
theoretical framework, TCER positioning, research questions, measurement
strategy, or how to contribute as a research collaborator — see the
[Research Framing](./research-framing.md).

## Community

We're building this in the open and we want your involvement:

- **Programming educators** — practitioner knowledge, classroom reality checks,
  what works and what doesn't
- **Learners** — willing to provide feedback, reflect on your learning process
- **Open-source education communities** — review, adaptation, translation
- **Coding bootcamp networks** — testing in intensive learning contexts
- **University CS departments** — experimenting with AI integration in formal
  courses
- **Self-taught programmer communities** — testing outside institutional
  contexts
